WWII Website (From a U.S. Perspective)

The War

Important Conferences

Casablanca Conference (January 14-24 1943) This conference involved only two of the “Big 3.” FDR and Churchill at Casablanca agreed on the strategy to win the war. This strategy included invading Sicily, next Italy. After Italy, the allies would invade France and increase bombing campaigns on Germany. The final term of Casablanca was “unconditional surrender” for the Axis Powers. Casablanca was essentially the outline for victory in Europe.

Tehran Conference (November 28- December 1 1943) Tehran involved all three members-Churchill, Roosevelt, and Stalin. These three agreed that the British and American would begin their drive into France in the Spring of 1944. Stalin and the Soviet Union would join the war once they got “unconditional surrender” from Germany. This Conference also planned the D-Day invasion.

Financing the War/ the Economy

The War Production Board (WPB) was established to manage the war industries in 1942. This would be similar to WW1 when the Federal Government organized agencies for this purpose. Government spending would increase by 1,000 % between 1939 and 1945. World War II would help the U.S. to spend its way out of the Depression, something the New Deal failed to prove. The gross national product would increase by 15 percent or more per year. By the end of the war, the U.S. would see a whooping $250 billion in debt.

Business and Industry would thrive in this time period. The depression would be pretty much over as unemployment was almost non-existent. U.S. industries exceeded their production and profits of the 20's. War-related industries pumped out so much output. Instead of producing automobiles, American factories made 300,000 planes, 100,000 tanks, and a capacity of 53 million tons of ships. This would be twice the production of all three of the Axis Power combined. Many would say this efficiency won the war. Smaller businesses would lose out on government contracts to larger corporations. The 100 largest corporations accounted for about 70% of wartime production.

To pay for this amount of Government spending, the government would perform an increase of income tax along with selling war bonds. Workers Union rights would also be limited during this time period. Labor unions and large corporations agreed to ban union strikes. The Smith Connally Act would enable the government to take over any war-related businesses who threatened to strike.

The war would change the lives and identities of women. Women would join the military but in non-combat roles. They would also join the workforce in factories while most men fought abroad. Almost 5 million women would enter the workforce. African Americans would leave the south in order to take jobs in the west and the north as well. American Indians and Mexican Americans would also contribute to the war effort. These groups would look to find together as “bands of brothers” either in combat or in the workforce for the common good of reducing prejudices.

From North Africa to D-Day

With Operation Torch, the allies began their daunting task of driving German forces out of North Africa. Starting in November of 1942, the allies led by General Eisenhower (United States) and General Bernard Montgomery (British) succeeded in taking North Africa from the Germans by May of 1943.

Next for the allies was Sicily, the Mediterranean island right by Italy. This was preparatory for taking over Italy. By the time the allies came for Italy, Mussolini fell from power. Italy would surrender and Germany would take over control of much of Northern Italy until Germany surrendered in 1945.

The final step towards German Surrender and Victory and Europe was the allied drive to liberate France. This began on June 6th, 1944 on D-Day. D-Day would be known as the largest invasion by sea in history. British, Canadian, and U.S. forces led by Eisenhower secured beachheads on the coast of Normandy. Many people died in this bloody attack. However, the allies would still move strong and rapidly to push back the German forces. The allies would cross the German Border for the final push to Berlin by September of 1944. The Nazi’s would launch a counterattack of desperation in the Battle of the Bulge. This created a bulge in allied forces but the allies reorganized and continued their advance into German lands.

V-E Day

The Germans would realize the end of the war was close. Hitler would commit suicide on April 30, 1945. A week later, the Nazi’s surrendered on May 7th. V-E Day, May 8th, 1945, marks the official end of the War in Europe. People across the world celebrated the end of war in Europe. When the United States advanced through Germany, they would discover the holocaust with the finding of the concentration camps. It would be a shock to Americans and the world that this was going on. The fact that 6 million Jews and several million non-Jews had been murdered under this system was a shock.

More Conferences/ FDR’s Death

Yalta Conference (February 1945) The Big 3 would meet once again in Yalta, a resort on the Black Sea coast of the Soviet Union. This meeting would be the most historic of the three conferences. After Victory in Europe was achieved, it was agreed that Germany would be divided into four occupation zones. Germany would be divided between the U.S., British, Russian, and France. Yalta also set the first meeting for the future United Nations in San Francisco in the spring of 1945.

FDR’s Death When FDR returned to the U.S after Yalta, he died all of a sudden when resting in Georgia. FDR’s death on April 12th, 1945 was as shocking as Pearl Harbor to the nation. The country was devastated as a whole. Truman would take over as Commander in Chief during a war that hadn’t been won.

Potsdam (July 17th- August 2nd, 1945) At Potsdam, only Stalin remained of the Big 3. Churchill lost his election at home and had to leave midway through Yalta and Clement Atlee took over as Prime Minister of Great Britain. Truman, Stalin and Atlee Agreed to demand Japanese unconditional surrender and hold war-crime trials of Nazi Leaders.

War in the Pacific

Along with the U.S., the British and Soviet forces would play a role in defeating Germany. In the Pacific however, It would be mainly U.S. forces to challenge the Japanese. By 1942, the Japanese would occupy much of East and Southeast Asia. The U.S. would intercept and decode Japanese messages and codes. This would help destroy four Japanese carriers and 300 planes in the Battle of Midway from June 4th to 7th. Midway would be a turning point because it would end Japanese expansion. After Midway, the U.S. began “Island Hopping” which was a campaign to get close to the mainland of Japan by occupying strategic positions in the Pacific. This would enable allied forces to move steadily to Japan.

Early in 1942, the Japanese conquered the Philippines and Eisenhower vowed that he would return and take back the Philippines. The battle for reoccupation of the Philippines would go down as the largest naval battle in history with the Battle of Leyte Gulf. In October 1944, the Japanese navy was basically destroyed. They responded with Kamikaze pilots to make suicide attacks on U.S. ships for the first time in the war. These Kamikazes would inflict major damage in the Battle of Okinawa. Before occupying the island successfully, the U.S. suffered 50,000 casualties but killed 100,000 Japanese.

Atomic Bomb, Japanese Surrender

The U.S. developed a destructive weapon in the Top-Secret Manhattan Project which had begun in 1942. This project led by physicist J. Robert Oppenheimer employed over 100,000 people and spent $2 billion to develop the weapon known to be the A-Bomb. This bomb would be tested in Alamogordo, New Mexico in the Trinity Test. President Truman and allies would call for Japan’s unconditional surrender or to face “utter destruction.” Japan wouldn’t surrender just yet even though surrender was near. Truman would ultimately decide to use the bomb. On August 6th, an A- Bomb would be dropped on Hiroshima and a second on Nagasaki on August 9th. Japan faced a prolonged period of suffering and 250,000 deaths.

The Japanese agreed to surrender a week later if they were allowed to keep the emperor in power as head of state. On the USS Missouri, General Douglas MacArthur received Japan’s formal surrender on September 2nd, 1945 off Tokyo harbor.

V-J Day

Victory in Japan day was September 2nd, 1945 (the day of Japanese surrender). This marked the end of a 4 year conflict in the Pacific. Millions celebrated as this brought relief to allied nations.
